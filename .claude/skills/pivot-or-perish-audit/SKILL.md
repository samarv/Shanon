# Pivot-or-Perish Audit

When a startup is neither growing rapidly nor failing completely, it becomes a "zombie"—an undead entity that consumes years of founder life without creating value. This framework uses Eric Ries's "first principles" approach to evaluate whether to stay the course, pivot the strategy, or shut down entirely.

## Phase 1: The Intellectual Honesty Test

Before looking at data, perform these two psychological resets to bypass the "reality distortion field."

### The "Magic Wand" Reset
Ask yourself and your co-founders: "If I could magically wave a wand and start a brand-new company today with my current team and capital, would I start *this* company?"
- **If No:** You are in a state of "indentured servitude" to your past self. A pivot or shutdown is mandatory.
- **If Yes:** Proceed to the data audit.

### The "Flat Hockey Stick" Audit
Review your business plan from 6–12 months ago.
- **The Pattern:** Does your current growth look like 10.5% → 10.6% → 10.65%? 
- **The Insight:** If you are "up and to the right" but at an insignificant slope that never hits the "stick," you are not on track; you are stuck in the law of diminishing returns. No amount of "polishing" will fix a fundamentally wrong value proposition.

## Phase 2: The 6-Week Pivot Sprint

If the audit suggests you are stuck, do not immediately shut down. Instead, execute a time-boxed experiment to find a productive paradigm.

### 1. The "Start Over" Brainstorm
Gather the team for a "zero-constraints" meeting. 
- **The Question:** "If we started this company over again today, knowing what we know now about the market, what is the one thing we wish we were doing?"
- **The Selection:** If the team aligns on a new direction, commit to it. If there are three competing ideas, assign one month to each in order of excitement.

### 2. Radical MVP Reduction
For the new direction, apply the "Rule of Halves" to your feature list:
- List all features deemed "necessary" for the new version.
- Cut that list in half.
- Cut it in half again.
- **The Goal:** Build only the one critical feature that tests the "Leap of Faith" assumption.

### 3. Establish the "Learning Metric"
Don't track total users or revenue (vanity metrics). Identify the one behavior that proves the new strategy works.
- *Example:* Instead of "signups," track "users who perform [X] action 3 times in the first week."

## Phase 3: The Governance Check

If the pivot requires a radical shift that investors or the board might block, use the "Fiduciary Duty to Flourishing" argument:
1. **Identify the Core Stakeholder:** Determine who the product *actually* serves (e.g., the customer, the community, the specific user).
2. **Present the Pivot as Value Preservation:** Argue that staying the course is a guaranteed destruction of capital and human potential, whereas the pivot aligns the fiduciary duty with the long-term survival of the "company's soul."

## Examples

**Example 1: The B2B "Feature Creep" Trap**
- **Context:** A team has spent 18 months building a complex data dashboard. They have 20 customers, but usage is low. They keep adding "quality" features.
- **Application:** The founder asks the "Magic Wand" question and realizes they hate the dashboard. In the "Start Over" brainstorm, the lead engineer reveals everyone actually loves a small internal script they wrote to automate one tiny part of the data flow.
- **Output:** They strip the product down to just that one script (the 6-week MVP), launch it as a standalone tool, and see a 10x spike in organic "Learning Metrics."

**Example 2: The "Zombie" Marketplace**
- **Context:** A marketplace for hiring experts has plateaued. Growth is 0.5% monthly.
- **Application:** The team realizes they are on the "flat part of the hockey stick." They set a 6-week deadline. They realize they are faking "quality" by having too many categories.
- **Output:** They pivot to a "Concierge MVP" where they manually match experts for just one niche category. If the "Learning Metric" (repeat bookings) doesn't move in 6 weeks, they agree to give the remaining capital back to investors.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Confusing Strategy with Vision:** A pivot is a change in *strategy* (how you get there), not a change in *vision* (why you exist). If you change the "why" every six months, you aren't pivoting; you're drifting.
- **Ego Identification:** Treating the company's failure as a personal death. You must dis-identify with the entity to make the rational decision to kill it or change it.
- **Lying in Investor Updates:** Sending "Everything is Great" updates while privately planning a shutdown. This prevents "Island of Freedom" thinking and blocks helpful feedback from stakeholders.
- **Waiting for "The Right Time":** Thinking you will pivot "after the next round" or "after this launch." In governance and pivots, it is always "too early" until it is suddenly "too late."
