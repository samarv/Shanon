# Sacred Cow Product Roadmap

The "Sacred Cow" approach transforms organizational taboos into a high-value innovation roadmap. In stagnant companies, "refining the core" often leads to a calcified culture where the most impactful changes are considered off-limits. By identifying these "sacred cows," you reveal exactly where the most significant opportunities for product evolution reside.

## The Process

### 1. Identify the Sacred Cows
Inventory the rules, features, or constraints that the organization believes are "not allowed" to be changed.
- Ask the team: "What are the things we are afraid to touch?"
- Look for features that have stayed the same for 5+ years despite shifts in user behavior.
- Identify "religious" constraints (e.g., "We will never have an edit button," "We must stay under X characters").

### 2. Staff for Obsession
Do not staff "sacred cow" projects based on availability. Staff them with "believers."
- Recruit internal entrepreneurs or use small acquihires (founders) who have a natural obsession with the problem.
- Ensure the lead has the "savviness" to navigate the system but the "will" to break it.
- **Rule**: If the team doesn't believe in the heresy, the project will fail at the first sign of friction.

### 3. Protect the "Speculative Bet" Silo
Sacred cow projects often hurt core metrics (like DAU or Revenue) in the short term. Protect them from standard performance frameworks.
- Create a "startup" environment for the team.
- Exempt them from quarterly OKRs that favor "refining the core."
- Judge success based on "learning" and "capability creation" rather than immediate top-line growth.

### 4. Apply the "Smell Test" for Trade-offs
When frameworks (like Jobs-to-be-Done or OKRs) suggest a decision that feels customer-hostile, prioritize "product taste."
- Ask: "Even if this drives the metric, do I hate this as a user?"
- If the framework leads to a subjectively bad experience (e.g., hiding order details to force a login), the framework is failing.
- Use leadership judgment to overrule metrics when a sacred cow project creates a long-term "vector for conversation" that the current product cannot support.

## Examples

### Example 1: Challenging Feed Structure
- **Context**: A social platform has used a reverse-chronological feed for a decade. Leadership is afraid to move to a ranked feed because "users will revolt."
- **Input**: User growth is declining; "refining the core" is no longer working.
- **Application**: Identify the "reverse-chron" as a sacred cow. Staff a small, isolated team to build an algorithmic ranking system. 
- **Output**: A ranked timeline that, despite initial friction, dramatically increases long-term retention and DAU.

### Example 2: Introducing User-Moderation
- **Context**: A platform believes only the company should moderate content; giving users control is seen as "dangerous to free speech."
- **Input**: Harassment is high, and the internal "policing" team cannot scale.
- **Application**: Identify "company-only moderation" as the sacred cow. Create a feature (e.g., "Hide Replies") that gives the original poster control over their space.
- **Output**: Users feel more empowered to share content, reducing the burden on central moderation.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Staffing by Availability**: Assigning a "sacred cow" project to a PM who is just "free" leads to lukewarm execution. These projects require irrational obsession to survive.
- **Religious Adherence to Frameworks**: Following Jobs-to-be-Done or OKRs so strictly that you ignore the "forest for the trees." If a framework prevents a big, bold bet because it doesn't fit a specific "job," discard the framework for that project.
- **Competing Internally**: Building a "new" version of a feature on a separate stack while the "core" team builds their own version. This creates political deadlock and technical debt.
- **Ignoring the Short-Term Dip**: Killing a project because it moved a core metric down (e.g., moving ads down to make room for a new feature). You must "squint" to see the long-term value.
