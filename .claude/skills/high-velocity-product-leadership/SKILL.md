# High-Velocity Product Leadership

In larger organizations, pace often degrades to match the recurring meeting calendar. High-velocity leadership involves "bending the universe" to your will by decoupling action from the calendar and setting an explicit expectation that the team operates "one click faster" than their natural inclination.

## Accelerating the Clock Speed

The core of fast execution is the "One Click Faster" rule. When a leader or team proposes a timeline, your role is to challenge the iteration unit.

### The "One Click" Shift
Apply this transformation to every proposed deadline:
*   **If it's a Year project:** It needs to be a Half project.
*   **If it's a Half project:** It needs to be a Quarter project.
*   **If it's a Quarter project:** It needs to be a Month project.
*   **If it's a Month project:** It needs to be a Week project.
*   **If it's a Week project:** It needs to be done Today.
*   **If it's for the Next Meeting:** It needs to be decided in This Meeting.

### Breaking the Calendar Dependency
*   **Decouple Next Steps from Syncs:** Refuse the phrase "We'll follow up on this in the next meeting." Instead, ask: "What specific information is missing to make this decision by tomorrow morning?"
*   **Set a Personal SLA:** As a leader, you are often the bottleneck for approvals or tie-breaking. Maintain a high-responsiveness SLA so the team never waits on you to maintain their "one click faster" pace.
*   **Reduce Coordination Headroom:** Avoid "coordination for coordination's sake." If a decision can be made with 70% certainty now, make it rather than waiting for the next alignment forum.

## Maintaining the High Bar

Velocity without quality is "feature factory" behavior. High-velocity organizations require extreme talent density and radical candor.

### Radical Candor ("Clear is Kind")
*   **Direct Confrontation:** When leaders are misaligned, do not "soft pedal" the feedback. Tell them: "The way you are operating is not meeting expectations. If it doesn't change, you cannot be part of this organization. I believe you can change, and I need to see it tomorrow."
*   **Separate Idea Crit from Person Crit:** Normalize a culture where questioning an idea is a service to the product, not an attack on the individual's talent.

### Measurable Career Ladders
Generic ladders (e.g., "Manages cross-functional stakeholders") lead to soft bars. Replace them with tractable, specific metrics:
*   **Poor:** "Excellent communicator."
*   **Better:** "Successfully pitches and secures budget for one net-new initiative per half."
*   **Poor:** "Strong technical partner."
*   **Better:** "Directly contributes to PRD/Spec parity with GitHub implementation; identifies architectural bottlenecks before they hit production."

## Examples

**Example 1: Pulling in a Roadmap**
*   **Context:** A Platform PM presents a H2 roadmap for a data migration.
*   **Input:** "We'll start discovery in Q3 and aim for completion by EOY."
*   **Application:** Apply the One Click Faster rule. Ask: "What would it take to finish the discovery by next Friday and have the first 10% of users migrated by the end of Q3?"
*   **Output:** The team identifies that "waiting for a security review meeting" is the bottleneck. The leader calls the CISO immediately to resolve it, pulling the project forward by two months.

**Example 2: Fixing Cross-Functional Friction**
*   **Context:** An Engineering Manager and PM are constantly debating priority, causing the dev team to stall.
*   **Input:** "They just have different styles and need more 1:1 time."
*   **Application:** Use "Clear is Kind." The leader tells both: "Your inability to align is costing the company velocity. This is a leadership failure. I need a unified plan on my desk by tomorrow at 9 AM, or we will restructure the leadership of this pod."
*   **Output:** The clear ultimatum forces the pair to resolve the "ego" friction and deliver a prioritized list, unblocking the engineers immediately.

## Common Pitfalls

*   **Waiting for the "GPT Store" equivalent:** Don't wait for the organization to provide the tools or permissions to move fast. Build the "wrapper" or the process yourself (the "scrappiness" mindset).
*   **Conflict Avoidance:** Managers often "soft pedal" performance issues to be "nice." This is unkind because it prevents the individual from improving and drags down the team's "clock speed."
*   **Solid-Line Thinking:** Staying strictly in your "lane" (e.g., "I'm a PM, I don't look at GitHub"). High-velocity leaders have "short toes" and traverse functional boundaries (Product, Engineering, Design) to unblock the path.
*   **The "Next Meeting" Trap:** Assuming that the weekly sync is the heartbeat of the project. The heartbeat should be the *work*, not the *calendar*.
